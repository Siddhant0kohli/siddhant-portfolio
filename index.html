Alzheimer‚Äôs Machine Learning Diagnosis Overview

View Certificate View Project

Context & Objective

This project explores how classical statistical analysis and supervised machine learning can be used to distinguish between Alzheimer‚Äôs and non-Alzheimer‚Äôs cases using structured clinical data. The focus was not on model complexity, but on understanding the data, its limitations, and the reliability of conclusions drawn from it.

Rather than treating models as black boxes, the goal was to examine which signals are present, how stable they are, and under what assumptions they remain valid. Multiple models were evaluated, with performance judged not only by accuracy, but by interpretability and robustness.

Statistical Analysis & EDA

This stage focused on understanding the structure, distribution and limitations of the clinical data before any modelling decisions were made.
  • Examining feature distributions, skewness and scale differences across clinical variables.
  • Normality testing using Shapiro‚ÄìWilk to guide appropriate statistical assumptions.
  • Outlier verification using Z-score and Tukey (IQR) methods, distinguishing data errors from genuine clinical variability.
  • Probability-based questions grounded in real clinical interpretation rather than abstract metrics.
  • Inferential testing including t-tests, chi-square tests and point-biserial correlation to assess feature relevance.

Modelling Approach

Modelling focused on comparing classical, interpretable machine-learning approaches rather than maximising algorithmic complexity, aligning with best practices in medical analytics.

The modelling pipeline included:

  • Feature selection was performed using mutual information combined with statistical relevance checks. Min‚ÄìMax scaling was applied to support distance-based and margin-based models.
  • Models included Logistic Regression, K-Nearest Neighbours, Decision Trees with controlled depth, and Support Vector Machines using a linear kernel.
  • Train‚Äìtest splits and k-fold cross-validation were used alongside hyperparameter tuning (GridSearchCV) to control overfitting and evaluate bias‚Äìvariance trade-offs.
  • Models were evaluated using accuracy, confusion matrices, classification reports and ROC‚ÄìAUC, with emphasis on stability and consistency across folds.

Key Results

  • Support Vector Machines performed best overall with 83% accuracy signalling roughly 8 out of 10 patients were classified correctly, While Logistic Regression achieved similar accuracy with the added benefit of being easier to interpret and explain.
  • Decision Trees tended to overfit the data when not carefully controlled, meaning they performed well on training data but were less reliable on new patients. Model behaviour remained consistent with earlier statistical tests and correlation findings.
  • Results were treated as decision-support signals rather than medical diagnoses, ensuring conclusions were cautious, evidence-based, and suitable for supporting (not replacing) clinical judgement.
  • The analysis also answered practical probability-based questions, such as the likelihood that a patient has both depression and diabetes, or the probability that a patient with a family history of Alzheimer‚Äôs also has hypertension.

Limitations and Learnings

Key Limitations

Limitations included reliance on secondary clinical data, limited longitudinal depth, potential self-reporting bias and sensitivity to preprocessing and feature definitions.

This project reinforced the importance of treating medical prediction tasks as statistical reasoning problems rather than purely optimisation exercises. Model performance alone is insufficient without understanding assumptions, data quality and generalisability. I learned that simpler, well-validated models (such as Logistic Regression and SVM) often outperform more complex alternatives when data is limited or noisy. Interpretability proved as important as accuracy.

Core Learnings

Simpler, statistically grounded models often outperform complex alternatives on limited data. Interpretability is as important as accuracy and models should support, not replace clinical judgements.

Saint Ann's School ‚Äî Expansion Strategy Overview

View Project

Context & Objective

This consulting project evaluated the feasibility of Saint Ann‚Äôs School establishing its first Australian campus. The objective was to identify a location that balances financial capacity, education demand, parental engagement, and cultural alignment with progressive international schooling models.

Rather than ranking suburbs purely by wealth, the analysis focused on actionable enrolment potential ‚Äî combining statistical validation, demographic structure and real-world schooling behaviour.

Methodology & Data Sources

Multi-Stage Statistical & Demographic Framework
  • Data sourced from ATO (taxable income, business income, superannuation) and ABS (GCP tables G15, G26, G29, G30, G32‚ÄìG56).
  • Suburb shortlisting using population thresholds and combined wealth metrics to ensure both financial capacity and enrolment scale.
  • Geographic clustering to reflect real school catchment behaviour rather than isolated postcode-level comparisons.
  • Conducted regular client meetings to clarify objectives, constraints, and success criteria, ensuring analysis remained aligned with real-world decision needs.
  • Translated qualitative client goals and stakeholder concerns into clear, testable statistical questions, guiding data selection, modelling choices, and interpretation.

The revised methodology shifted from ‚Äúrichest suburbs‚Äù to statistically defensible enrolment zones, integrating financial capacity with demographic demand and parental behaviour.

Analysis & Key Findings

  • Initial descriptive filtering followed by ANOVA and Tukey post-hoc tests confirmed NSW and VIC as statistically wealthier than other states.
  • Pure wealth did not guarantee enrolment viability ‚Äî several ultra-wealthy suburbs lacked scale.
  • Private school penetration proved a stronger demand signal than income alone.
  • ABS indicators showed strong links between parental education, profession and school choice.
  • Rising non-government enrolments nationally reinforced market readiness.

These findings reframed the problem from identifying affluent areas to isolating where wealth, scale, and schooling behaviour intersect.

Target Clustering

  • Northern Sydney: Family-engaged, multicultural, strong unpaid childcare signals.
  • Eastern Sydney: Prestige-driven, exceptionally high private school uptake.
  • Melbourne Toorak: Ultra-wealthy but limited student volume.
  • Cluster formation avoided isolated postcode bias.
  • Education demand, transport accessibility, and professional density validated.

Sydney emerged with two scalable, distinct demand clusters, while Melbourne‚Äôs wealth failed to translate into sustainable enrolment scale.

Final Recommendation

Recommended Entry Location: Eastern Sydney
  • ~14,300 students aged 5‚Äì17 with exceptionally high private school penetration.
  • Private enrolment rates up to 82% in key suburbs.
  • Strong professional density (law, finance, medicine).
  • Globally oriented parent base aligned with international education models.
  • Lower adoption risk relative to other clusters.

Eastern Sydney offers the strongest balance of prestige, scale, willingness-to-pay, and cultural alignment, making it the optimal launch location for Saint Ann‚Äôs.

Limitations and Learnings

Key Limitations

The analysis relied on publicly available ATO and ABS datasets, which aggregate income and demographic indicators at suburb and regional levels. As a result, the model may under-represent private wealth concentration, intergenerational assets, and short-term migration or policy-driven demand shifts. The findings therefore describe structural enrolment potential rather than guaranteed demand, and are best interpreted as a shortlisting and prioritisation framework rather than a final site-selection decision.

Core Learnings

This project reinforced that wealth alone is an insufficient decision variable in education expansion. High income does not automatically translate into enrolment intent, scale, or long-term sustainability. More reliable signals emerged from the interaction between demographic structure, parental behaviour, education preferences, and cultural norms, particularly where these factors aligned spatially.

Portfolio Optimisation ‚Äî Overview

View Project

Context & Objective

This project applied linear, integer, and non-linear optimisation models to construct an investment portfolio across multiple sectors, balancing return, risk, diversification, and practical investment constraints.

Rather than maximising returns in isolation, the analysis treated portfolio construction as a constraint-driven decision problem, explicitly modelling sector exposure, risk categories, and capital allocation rules to reflect realistic investment behaviour.

Methodology & Model Design

  • Selected 10 Australian stocks across five sectors: Basic Materials, Healthcare, Financials, Technology, and Industrials.
  • Monthly price data (Feb 2021 ‚Äì Feb 2024) used to compute average returns and volatility.
  • Stocks classified into four risk categories (R1‚ÄìR4) based on standard deviation of returns.
  • Decision variables defined as portfolio weights for each stock.
  • Constraints enforced sector exposure, risk balance, and full capital allocation.
  • Optimisation implemented using Excel Solver for LP, ILP, and NLP formulations.

The modelling framework prioritised mathematical transparency and interpretability, allowing each constraint and allocation decision to be directly traced to an investment rationale.

Optimisation & Key Results

  • Linear Programming (LP): Maximised average portfolio return under sector and risk constraints, producing a diversified allocation anchored by Technology One, BHP, CSL, and Transurban.
  • Integer Linear Programming (ILP): Selected exactly eight stocks with equal weighting, achieving the highest average return (~8.82%) while satisfying category and risk constraints.
  • Non-Linear Programming (Risk-Bound): Maximised return subject to a 4% portfolio risk cap, revealing diminishing returns at higher risk tolerance.
  • Non-Linear Programming (Return-Bound): Minimized risk while enforcing a minimum 1.5% return, identifying an efficient moderate-risk portfolio.
  • Sharpe Ratio Optimisation: Highlighted sensitivity to the assumed risk-free rate, producing a negative Sharpe under historical conditions.

Across models, results demonstrated that constraint design materially shapes portfolio behaviour. Higher nominal returns often emerged from tighter structural rules rather than unconstrained optimisation.

Limitations and Learnings

Key Limitations

The optimisation models relied on historical return and volatility estimates, which may not persist under changing market conditions. Transaction costs, liquidity constraints, and dynamic correlations were excluded, and linear formulations cannot fully capture non-linear market behaviour or regime shifts.

Sharpe ratio outcomes were highly sensitive to the assumed risk-free rate, illustrating how small modelling assumptions can materially alter conclusions.

Core Learnings

This project reinforced that portfolio optimisation is a decision-support exercise rather than a forecasting tool. The true value lies in understanding trade-offs between risk, return, diversification, and constraint design ‚Äî not in identifying a single ‚Äúoptimal‚Äù allocation. Well-structured constraints often produced more realistic and robust portfolios than unconstrained maximisation, highlighting the importance of aligning mathematical models with real investment logic.

NLP Topic Modelling Overview

View Project

Context & Objective

This project examined whether unsupervised NLP techniques can uncover meaningful, stable themes within large-scale social media data. Using Twitter discussions around ChatGPT, the objective was to evaluate topic modelling as a statistical reasoning tool.

Rather than treating topic models as black boxes, the analysis focused on data quality, modelling assumptions, topic stability, and interpretability. Both sentiment analysis and topic modelling were used to understand how users discuss AI, education, business, and technology.

Data Collection & Pre-processing

This stage focused on preparing noisy social media text for reliable NLP analysis.
  • Twitter dataset containing multilingual tweets related to ChatGPT and AI.
  • Filtered to English-language tweets for sentiment and topic modelling.
  • Removed URLs, emojis, punctuation, HTML artefacts, and non-textual noise.
  • Lowercasing, tokenisation, stopword removal, and text normalisation.
  • Noun-based filtering and term-frequency thresholds to reduce sparsity.

Exploratory Text Analysis

  • Language distribution analysis revealed English as the dominant medium.
  • Tweet volume peaked sharply around major ChatGPT news events.
  • Hashtag frequency analysis highlighted strong AI, OpenAI, Microsoft and education themes.
  • Identification of highly active users and most retweeted posts.
  • Initial signals of thematic clustering prior to formal modelling.

Topic Modelling Approach

Topic modelling was treated as a probabilistic statistical exercise rather than a purely NLP task.
  • Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) used for unsupervised topic discovery.
  • Bag-of-words representation with controlled vocabulary size.
  • Iterative experimentation with topic counts.
  • Coherence score (cv) used to select the optimal number of topics.
  • Seven topics chosen as the best balance between coherence and interpretability.

Key Results

  • Distinct themes emerged around education, business use, coding, employment, and AI ethics.
  • Highest-probability topics reflected debates on ChatGPT passing professional exams.
  • Sentiment analysis showed OpenAI receiving the strongest positive sentiment.
  • Topics were probabilistic distributions rather than hard classifications.
  • Results aligned closely with real-world discourse around AI adoption and disruption.

Limitations and Learnings

Key Limitations

Topic modelling results are highly sensitive to preprocessing choices, vocabulary selection, and modelling assumptions. LDA‚Äôs bag-of-words framework ignores word order and contextual semantics, which limits its ability to capture nuance in short social media texts. Topics should therefore be interpreted as statistical patterns, not definitive labels.

Core Learnings

This project reinforced that NLP models are tools for exploration and sense-making, not sources of absolute truth. Careful preprocessing, coherence-driven validation, and domain interpretation are essential for meaningful results. Statistical reasoning and human judgement remain central to interpreting unsupervised NLP outputs.
